# Neuroscience for a Continual-Learning Memory-Consolidation Agent

Research notes informing the dream-state project. Project mappings used throughout:
`currency = reward function`, `PFC = policy network`, `dopamine = training signal`,
`hippocampus = memory writer`, `cortex = long-term store`, `sleep = consolidation phase`.

Each section gives the **mechanism**, **key papers**, **consensus vs. speculation**, and a
**design mapping** to the AI architecture (modules: {thinking, long-term memory, short-term
memory, context composition, policy, reward}).

## 1. Complementary Learning Systems (CLS): hippocampus ↔ neocortex

### 1.1 The framework
The brain solves the tension between (a) learning specific new experiences fast and (b)
accumulating structured, generalizable knowledge slowly without destroying old knowledge, by
using **two complementary learning systems**.

- **Neocortex = slow, interleaved learner** (the AI "long-term store"). Overlapping distributed
  representations, small learning rate. Discovers statistical structure/schemas. *Requires
  interleaved training* — new data must be mixed with old, or it catastrophically interferes.
  Inherently slow.
- **Hippocampus = fast, sparse learner** (the AI "memory writer"). Sparse, **pattern-separated**
  codes (dentate gyrus) so new episodes don't collide; CA3 recurrent collaterals do
  autoassociative **pattern completion**. One-shot encoding without corrupting cortex.
- **Resolution:** hippocampus stores the episode, then *repeatedly reactivates* it to cortex,
  supplying the interleaved "training examples" cortex needs — the theoretical bridge to sleep
  consolidation.

**Founding paper:** McClelland, McNaughton & O'Reilly (1995), *Psychological Review* 102(3):419–457,
"Why There Are Complementary Learning Systems in the Hippocampus and Neocortex." Turned
*catastrophic interference* (McCloskey & Cohen 1989; Ratcliff 1990) from a neural-net bug into a
principled argument for brain organization.

**2016 update (written for the AI/agent audience):** Kumaran, Hassabis & McClelland (2016),
*Trends in Cognitive Sciences* 20(7):512–534, "What Learning Systems do Intelligent Agents Need?
CLS Theory Updated." Two revisions that matter directly for this project:
1. **Replay need not be veridical or random** — the hippocampus can do **weighted, selective
   replay** prioritizing rewarding/surprising/goal-relevant experiences (statistically more
   efficient). This is the conceptual parent of *prioritized experience replay* in deep RL and of
   DeepMind's DQN experience replay.
2. **The hippocampus can generalize** — schema-consistent information can integrate rapidly
   (Tse et al. 2007), and the hippocampus supports inference by recombining stored elements.

> This single paper is the tightest bridge between the neuroscience and a wake–sleep consolidation
> agent with a learned routing policy. **Highest-priority citation for the project.**

### 1.2 Systems consolidation (hippocampus → cortex transfer)
Time-dependent reorganization: memories initially hippocampus-dependent become (for some types)
cortex-dependent over days–years. Distinct from *synaptic/cellular* consolidation (molecular
stabilization within hours). Classic evidence: temporally-graded retrograde amnesia after
hippocampal damage.

Two camps — **this is a genuinely live debate**:
- **Standard Model (SMSC)** — Squire & Alvarez (1995); Marr (1971) ancestor. Hippocampus is a
  *temporary* store; repeated reactivation strengthens cortico-cortical links until the memory is
  fully hippocampus-independent, even for episodic detail. Predicts time-limited hippocampal role.
- **Multiple Trace Theory → Trace Transformation Theory** — Nadel & Moscovitch (1997);
  Winocur, Moscovitch & Bontempi (2010). Detailed *episodic* memories *always* need the
  hippocampus; only **gist/semantic** extractions become cortical. TTT (the modern synthesis):
  consolidation is **transformation**, not transfer — a detailed hippocampal trace is transformed
  into a schematic, gist-like cortical version, and **both coexist**, expressed per retrieval
  demand.

**Consensus:** Semantic/gist → cortex is broadly agreed. Whether *detailed episodic* memory ever
becomes fully hippocampus-independent is **contested/open** — no clean experimental adjudication;
results hinge on how "episodic detail" is measured.

### 1.3 Sharp-wave ripples (SWRs)
Most synchronous population events in the mammalian brain. Two coupled components: a **sharp wave**
(CA3 Schaffer-collateral input to CA1 dendrites) and a **ripple** (~140–250 Hz rodent, ~80–140 Hz
human; pyramidal–PV-interneuron loop). Generated by the **CA3 recurrent network** (Buzsáki 2015,
*Hippocampus* 25(10):1073–1188, the definitive review); CA2 can act as an initiation zone
(Oliva et al. 2016, *Neuron*).

**When:** non-REM/slow-wave sleep and quiet/consummatory wakefulness (low acetylcholine, low
theta). Suppressed during active locomotion and REM.

**Causal role — bidirectional evidence (the gold standard):**
- **Loss-of-function:** Girardeau et al. (2009), *Nature Neuroscience* 12(10):1222–1223. Closed-loop
  suppression of ripples during post-training sleep impaired spatial memory. (Ego-Stengel & Wilson
  2010 convergent.)
- **Gain-of-function:** Fernández-Ruiz et al. (2019), *Science* 363(6429):1054–1059. **Prolonging**
  ripples improved memory; truncating them impaired it. Ripple *duration/content* is behaviorally
  consequential.

**Consensus:** Strong / near-consensus that SWRs are a causal substrate of consolidation. Contested
edges: detection standards (2022 *Nat. Commun.* consensus statement); whether *awake* ripples are
necessary across all tasks (Jung et al. 2023, *eLife* — task/state-dependent).

### 1.4 Replay
Time-compressed re-expression, during SWRs, of ordered place-cell firing sequences from prior
waking trajectories. The *content* SWRs often carry.

- Wilson & McNaughton (1994, *Science*) — sleep reactivation; Lee & Wilson (2002) — ordered SWS
  sequence replay.
- **Reverse replay** — Foster & Wilson (2006), *Nature* 440:680–683. At awake reward pauses,
  sequences replay backward → candidate mechanism for **reward credit assignment** (biological
  analogue of TD eligibility traces).
- **Forward & reverse in ripples** — Diba & Buzsáki (2007), *Nat. Neurosci.* 10:1241–1242.
  Forward = retrieval/planning; reverse = learning from outcome.

**2010s–2020s nuance (important):** Replay is **not** a literal readout of recent experience. Gupta
et al. (2010, *Neuron*) — replay includes **never-experienced shortcuts**, implicating
inference/planning/generalization. Pfeiffer & Foster (2013, *Nature*) — forward replay depicts
*upcoming* goal paths. Gillespie et al. (2021, *Neuron*) — awake replay preferentially represents
*previously rewarded* locations. Modern view: replay is flexible, selective, constructive, serving
consolidation + retrieval + credit assignment + planning + generalization, task/state-dependent.
Methodological caution: replay detection without ground truth can be a decoding artifact
(van der Meer et al.; Tingley & Peyrache 2020).

### 1.5 Slow oscillation–spindle–ripple coupling (NREM "triple coupling")
The dominant mechanistic account of *how* sleep implements CLS: hierarchical nesting of three NREM
oscillations (**active systems consolidation**, Klinzing, Niethard & Born 2019, *Nat. Neurosci.*
22(10):1598–1610):
1. **Neocortical slow oscillations (<1 Hz)** — the master clock, imposing Down/Up states.
2. **Thalamocortical spindles (~10–16 Hz)** — nested in the SO Up-state; open cortical plasticity
   windows.
3. **Hippocampal ripples** — nested in spindle troughs, delivering replayed content into the
   plastic cortical window.

The logic: the SO synchronizes the dialogue so **replayed memory (ripples) arrives when cortex is
in a plastic, spindle-gated Up-state**, redistributing memory to cortex.

**Causal support (strongest):** Maingret et al. (2016, *Nat. Neurosci.*) — reinforcing
ripple↔cortical-slow-wave coupling enhanced memory. Latchoumane et al. (2017, *Neuron*) —
optogenetic spindles phase-locked to the SO Up-state improved memory *only when properly timed*.
Human triple coupling: Staresina et al. (2015, *Nat. Neurosci.*); human intracranial (2023,
*Nat. Neurosci.*).

**Consensus:** Strong, with causal closed-loop support. Open: exact necessity/direction of each
component, coupling *precision* vs *count*, generalization beyond spatial/declarative tasks.

### 1.6 Consensus map
| Claim | Status |
|---|---|
| Fast hippocampal / slow cortical division (CLS core) | **Consensus** |
| Pattern separation (DG) vs completion (CA3) | Strong; details debated |
| SWRs causal for consolidation (bidirectional) | **Strong / near-consensus** |
| Replay exists, rides on SWRs | **Established** |
| Replay = flexible/selective/constructive | Emerging consensus; specifics debated |
| SO–spindle–ripple coupling as mechanism | **Strong** (causal support) |
| Detailed episodic memory ever fully cortex-only? (SMSC vs MTT/TTT) | **Contested / open** |
| Awake-ripple necessity across all tasks | **Contested** |

### 1.7 Design mapping
- Two-store split {short-term / long-term} ≈ CLS. Fast writer commits episodes; slow store
  integrates via interleaved replay to avoid catastrophic forgetting.
- Sleep/consolidation phase should do **prioritized, selective replay** (Kumaran 2016), not uniform
  rehearsal.
- Staresina 2015 / Klinzing 2019 give a biologically-motivated **consolidation scheduler**: replay
  content into the long-term store during structured "up-state" windows.

## 2. Two kinds of attention: gating INTO working memory vs operating ON its contents

**Core answer: the distinction is real and multiply supported — but best understood as a
three-way decomposition (input gate / maintenance / output gate), not a clean two-way split.**

Convergent traditions: cognitive taxonomy (Chun), computational neuroscience (PBWM), human fMRI
(McNab & Klingberg; Chatham & Badre), formal WM theory (Cowan, Oberauer).

### 2.1 The external/internal taxonomy
Chun, Golomb & Turk-Browne (2011), *Annual Review of Psychology* 62:73–101, "A Taxonomy of External
and Internal Attention."
- **External attention** = selecting/modulating *sensory* input (perceptual gating — partly "what
  gets in").
- **Internal attention** = selecting/modulating *internally generated* info: WM contents, task
  rules, LTM, response selection.
- Strong claim (Kiyonaga & Egner 2013): **WM maintenance IS internal attention** — "attention
  sustained internally over time." Influential but contested by dedicated-buffer (Baddeley) camp.

Note: Chun's axis (percept vs memory) *cross-cuts* the project's axis (admit vs manipulate). The
finer (a)/(b) distinction comes from the gating and focus-of-attention literatures below.

### 2.2 Basal-ganglia WM gating — the PBWM model (the mechanistic heart of "what enters")
- Frank, Loughry & O'Reilly (2001), *Cogn. Affect. Behav. Neurosci.* 1:137–160. BG implement
  **dynamic gating by disinhibition**: a direct-pathway **"Go"** signal (striatum → GPi → thalamus)
  disinhibits a fronto-thalamic loop → *update* PFC; **"NoGo"** (indirect pathway) → robust
  *maintenance*. Puts the update decision in a *separate, learnable* controller, not in PFC.
- O'Reilly & Frank (2006), *Neural Computation* 18:283–328, "Making Working Memory Work" — full
  PBWM. PFC organized into independently-updatable **stripes** (selective, not global, gating). The
  gating policy is **learned via dopaminergic RL** (actor-critic/TD) — "banishes the homunculus":
  no overseer needed, a trainable gate suffices.

**Consensus:** BG disinhibitory gating of fronto-thalamic loops is well-grounded in anatomy and is
consensus. Discrete PFC "stripes" and the specific learning rule are model idealizations.

### 2.3 Input vs output gating, and dopamine
The refined **three-way** scheme that directly answers the project's question:
- **Input gating (write)** = admit new info → this is "select what enters" (b).
- **Maintenance** = recurrent PFC holding contents.
- **Output gating (read)** = select which held item drives downstream processing → a mechanized
  form of "operate on / select among current contents" (a).

Dopamine gating hypothesis — Braver & Cohen (2000), "On the control of control"; Cohen, Braver &
O'Reilly (1996). **Phasic dopamine** transiently opens the gate to update PFC *and* carries a
reward-prediction-error learning signal that trains *when* to gate (conceptual parent of PBWM).
**D1/D2 stability–flexibility** (Cools & D'Esposito 2011, *Biol. Psychiatry*): D1 deepens attractors
→ maintenance; D2 destabilizes → updating; performance follows an **inverted-U** in dopamine level.

**Human evidence:**
- Input gating: **McNab & Klingberg (2008), *Nat. Neurosci.* 11:103–107** — prefrontal + **globus
  pallidus** activity *precedes and predicts* distractor filtering, correlating with WM capacity.
  Strongest direct evidence that a fronto-BG gate controls *access to* WM.
- Output gating: **Chatham, Frank & Badre (2014), *Neuron* 81:930–942** — selecting which held item
  drives the response recruits lateral PFC + fronto-striatal (caudate) coupling along a
  rostro-caudal gradient. Reading *out of* WM is a separately gated operation.

### 2.4 Retrieval of LTM into WM, and the "focus of attention"
The finest-grained account of "operating on current contents," blurring the boundary with retrieval
from LTM.
- **Cowan** embedded-processes model (2001, "The magical number 4," *BBS* 24): WM = activated subset
  of LTM; a narrower **focus of attention** holds ~4 chunks. Retrieval-from-LTM and
  attending-within-WM are nested levels of activation over one substrate.
- **Oberauer (2002), *JEP:LMC* 28:411–421** — three concentric states: (1) **activated LTM**,
  (2) **region of direct access** (~4 bound chunks = current WM contents), (3) **focus of
  attention** (single-item selector for the next operation). *This formalizes (a) vs (b):* moving an
  item from activated-LTM into the region of direct access = **select-into (b)**; moving the focus
  onto one chunk already in the region = **operate-on (a)**.
- **Retro-cue** paradigm (Griffin & Nobre 2003) — a cue *after* the stimulus is gone still boosts
  the cued WM item → attention oriented purely *internally*. Neural signature: alpha (~8–12 Hz)
  lateralization; and retro-cueing an internal item **impairs concurrent perceptual detection** →
  internal and external attention share a limited resource (Souza & Oberauer 2016, review).

### 2.5 Attention Schema Theory (the meta-level controller)
Graziano & Webb (2015), *Frontiers in Psychology* 6:500. Attention = **biased competition**
(Desimone & Duncan 1995) for limited processing resources — an allocatable resource. The **attention
schema** is a simplified internal *model* of one's own attention (analogous to the body schema for
motor control): a model of attention **improves top-down control** of it. Strong identity claim
(awareness = the attention schema) is philosophically contested, not settled neuroscience. Most
relevant framing for a routing-policy project: a **learned, model-based controller over a limited
workspace**.

### 2.6 Verdict + design mapping
The (a) vs (b) distinction is real (double dissociation of gates; anatomically separate entry gate;
retro-cue effects; formal state models). Caveat: they are **dissociable operations sharing
resources**, not separate organs; LTM→WM retrieval is a graded hybrid.

| Operation | Category | Mechanism | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Admit percept/LTM item into WM | **(b) select-into** | BG input gate (Go), phasic DA, D2 | McNab & Klingberg 2008; PBWM |
| Hold contents | maintenance | recurrent PFC, D1, NoGo | O'Reilly & Frank 2006 |
| Prioritize/read out held item | **(a) operate-on** | output gate + focus of attention | Chatham 2014; Griffin & Nobre 2003; Oberauer 2002 |

> **Directly transferable:** the input-gate (write) / maintenance / output-gate (read) triad,
> learned by a dopamine-like RL signal, is the cleanest neuroscience framing for a learned routing
> policy over the "context composition" + "short-term memory" modules. **AST** supplies the
> model-based-controller layer over that gating. The project's intuition of "attention that selects
> what enters WM" (input gate) vs "attention that operates on current thoughts" (output gate / focus
> of attention) is scientifically defensible.

## 3. Dopamine and memory consolidation: reward decides which memories persist

Strong, converging evidence that dopamine is a "salience/reward currency" gating which hippocampal
memories stabilize into long-term storage, at two timescales: (1) around encoding, via LTP
enhancement + synaptic tagging; (2) offline, via reward-biased replay.

### 3.1 The VTA–hippocampus loop
Lisman & Grace (2005), *Neuron* 46(5):703–713, "The Hippocampal-VTA Loop: Controlling the Entry of
Information into Long-Term Memory."
- **Ascending arm:** hippocampus (CA1/subiculum comparator) detects **novelty** → subiculum →
  nucleus accumbens → ventral pallidum → **disinhibits VTA dopamine neurons**. Novelty releases the
  brake.
- **Descending arm:** VTA dopamine → hippocampus, acts on **D1/D5** to enhance LTP and the
  early→late (protein-synthesis-dependent) LTP transition — enhancing memory *persistence*.
- Implements a decision about *entry of information into long-term memory*: novel/salient events
  open the gate.

**Refinement (debated):** much of the dopamine driving dorsal-hippocampal persistence may come from
the **locus coeruleus** (co-releasing dopamine, not just norepinephrine) — Takeuchi et al. (2016,
*Nature*); Kempadoo et al. (2016, *PNAS*). The robust part is the *signal* (dopamine on D1/D5), not
strictly its VTA source. Human support: Wittmann et al. (2005); Bunzeck & Düzel (2006).

### 3.2 Reward-tagged / prioritized encoding
- **Adcock et al. (2006), *Neuron* 50(3):507–517** — high- vs low-reward cue before each scene;
  high-reward scenes better remembered at 24h. **Anticipatory** VTA/NAcc/hippocampus activity
  (before the scene) predicted later memory — reward primes the circuit *in advance*.
- **Gruber, Gelman & Ranganath (2014), *Neuron*** — curiosity engaged the SN/VTA–hippocampus circuit
  and enhanced memory even for **incidental** info during high-curiosity states (dopaminergic states
  "spill over" to tag nearby weak memories).
- Framework: Gruber & Ranganath (2019, *TiCS*) "PACE" (Prioritizing memory Access, Consolidation,
  Encoding). **Consensus:** robust and replicated in humans.

### 3.3 Synaptic Tagging and Capture (STC) — the key mechanism for the AI analogy
- **Frey & Morris (1997), *Nature* 385:533–536.** Weak stimulation sets a transient, synapse-specific
  **"tag"** (early-LTP) but no plasticity-related proteins (PRPs). Strong stimulation elsewhere on
  the same neuron synthesizes **PRPs**. Within a time window, the tagged weak synapse **captures**
  PRPs → converts to persistent late-LTP. Solves specificity + persistence simultaneously.
- **Behavioral tagging** — Moncada & Viola (2007), *J. Neurosci.* 27(28):7476–7481. Weak training
  (short-term memory only) + independent salient **novelty** within ~±1h → memory lasts 24h+;
  **protein-synthesis- and D1/D5-dependent**. Novelty donates PRPs; weak learning sets the tag.
- Review: **Redondo & Morris (2011), *Nat. Rev. Neurosci.* 12:17–30.** Neuromodulation (dopamine,
  noradrenaline) triggers PRP synthesis — a heterosynaptic, cell-wide, **resource-limited,
  competitive** process: a weak memory survives only if a salient event nearby releases dopamine and
  makes PRPs in time.

**Consensus vs speculation:** STC (electrophysiology) and behavioral tagging (behavior) are
well-replicated. The **molecular identity of the tag and PRPs is unresolved** (candidates: PKMζ,
Arc, Homer1a, BDNF, CaMKII); no direct molecular observation of capture — the main open front.

### 3.4 Prioritized replay of rewarded experiences (offline) — direct analog of prioritized ER
- **Singer & Frank (2009), *Neuron* 64:910–921** — SWR reactivation stronger on **rewarded** vs
  error trials; reward itself drives reactivation.
- **Ambrose, Pfeiffer & Foster (2016), *Neuron* 91:1124–1136** — increasing reward selectively
  increased **reverse** replay (not forward); decreasing reward decreased it. Reverse replay as a
  **neural eligibility trace** credit-assigning value backward (close to TD learning).
- **Gomperts, Kloosterman & Wilson (2015), *eLife* 4:e05360** — reward-responsive VTA neurons co-fire
  with hippocampal SWR replay, signaling *where* rewards were found.
- **McNamara et al. (2014), *Nat. Neurosci.* 17:1658–1660** — optogenetic VTA-dopamine stimulation in
  CA1 during learning **increased post-learning replay and improved memory persistence** without
  changing learning itself. Cleanest causal demo that dopamine biases *which* memory consolidates.
- **Michon et al. (2019), *Current Biology* 29:1436–1444** — ripple-triggered disruption selectively
  impaired memory for **large-reward** locations; replay proportion scaled with reward size.

**Consensus:** reward enhances offline reactivation and benefits memory — well-supported and causal.
The finer claim that dopamine during replay *directly implements* value/credit assignment is a
compelling but still partly theoretical model.

### 3.5 Emotional/salience tagging (parallel "currency")
McGaugh (2000, *Science*; 2004, *Annu. Rev. Neurosci.*): emotional arousal → stress hormones →
**basolateral amygdala** → **norepinephrine** (+ ACh) modulates consolidation in hippocampus/cortex.
The amygdala doesn't store the declarative memory; it **biases which memories get consolidated**.
Converges with STC (NE/β-adrenergic sets/strengthens tags and drives PRP synthesis). Dopamine
(reward/novelty) and norepinephrine (arousal/emotion) are two priority signals feeding a shared
plasticity-stabilization machinery.

### 3.6 Design mapping
| Neuroscience | AI / agent analog |
|---|---|
| Dopamine as reward/novelty currency gating memory entry (Lisman & Grace 2005; Adcock 2006) | Learned **routing/write policy** using reward or surprise to decide which experiences the memory-writer commits to long-term store |
| Synaptic tagging & capture (Frey & Morris 1997; Moncada & Viola 2007) | **Deferred/credit-based commitment**: recent weak traces consolidated retroactively if a high-value event occurs nearby in time — an eligibility trace for *what to store* |
| Reward-biased reverse replay in sleep (Ambrose 2016; McNamara 2014; Michon 2019) | **Prioritized Experience Replay** (Schaul et al. 2016, ICLR) — sample transitions ∝ TD-error/value; a sleep phase draining a priority-weighted buffer |
| Novelty opens the memory gate | **Surprise/novelty prioritization** (curiosity, prediction-error) complementing extrinsic reward |

> Transferable insight: biology uses a **two-phase, resource-limited, priority-weighted** scheme — a
> fast online *tag* + a slow offline *capture/replay* spending limited consolidation on
> reward/novelty-tagged traces. Exactly the design space for a wake–sleep memory-writer with a
> learned routing policy.

## 4. Subconscious vs conscious processing

Modern cognitive neuroscience *does* support a defensible distinction between (a) massively parallel
unconscious/preconscious processing and (b) a capacity-limited, serially-accessed "conscious" state
into which selected info is broadcast. The dominant mechanistic account is **Global (Neuronal)
Workspace Theory**.

**Two caveats up front:**
- "**Subconscious**" is a Freudian/folk term, not neuroscientific. The literature says
  *unconscious / subliminal / preconscious*. There is **no single anatomical "subconscious
  decision-maker"** — the gating is distributed and largely attentional, not a homunculus.
- *Which* theory is correct is actively contested (GNW vs IIT vs Higher-Order); the 2025 adversarial
  test challenged all leading accounts. The *existence* of an unconscious→conscious transition is
  solid; the *mechanism* is where science shades into dispute.

### 4.1 Global Workspace Theory (GWT) — Baars (1988)
A **"blackboard" architecture** (borrowed from AI): many parallel unconscious specialist processors
compete (**winner-take-all**) to post content to a limited-capacity **global workspace**; the winner
is **broadcast** back to all processors → "globally available" = conscious access. The theater
metaphor: a spotlight of attention on the stage of WM, broadcast to the unconscious audience.
- Baars (1988), *A Cognitive Theory of Consciousness*; Baars (2005), *Prog. Brain Res.* 150:45–53.
- **Most defensible neuroscience→AI analogy for a "context composition"/broadcast module** — GWT was
  itself AI-derived (Hearsay-II; cf. Franklin's LIDA, Blum & Blum "Conscious Turing Machine"). But
  it is a **functional/computational** claim, not a claim of phenomenal consciousness.

### 4.2 Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW) — Dehaene, Changeux, Naccache
Neurobiological implementation: deep-layer pyramidal neurons with **long-range axons** (dense in
**prefrontal, parietal, cingulate**) form a workspace network. Sensory input is processed
unconsciously (feedforward); it becomes conscious only on **"ignition"** —
- Recurrent amplification crosses a **threshold** → self-sustaining, reverberating, distributed
  assembly that amplifies, maintains, and **broadcasts** the representation brain-wide.
- Signatures: **late amplification (~250–300 ms+)**, long-distance beta/gamma synchrony, a
  **sudden all-or-none nonlinear transition**, prefronto-parietal engagement.
- **The ~300 ms / P3b marker:** Sergent, Baillet & Dehaene (2005), *Nat. Neurosci.* 8:1391–1400
  (attentional blink). Early components (P1/N1) identical seen-vs-unseen; divergence ~270 ms with a
  **bimodal/all-or-none P3b** only for consciously perceived stimuli.
- Reviews: Dehaene & Changeux (2011), *Neuron* 70(2):200–227; **Mashour, Roelfsema, Changeux &
  Dehaene (2020), *Neuron* 105(5):776–798** (most current authoritative GNW review).

**Consensus:** Ignition/threshold and late prefronto-parietal amplification are robust, replicated.
Contested: whether prefrontal ignition *is* the conscious experience or a downstream consequence
tied to report/attention/WM (the "no-report" critique).

### 4.3 The subliminal/preconscious/conscious taxonomy — Dehaene et al. (2006)
*Trends in Cognitive Sciences* 10(5):204–211. **Two orthogonal factors**: bottom-up stimulus
strength × top-down attentional amplification.
- **Subliminal** — too weak to ignite; can prime but is *never* consciously accessible, even if
  attended.
- **Preconscious** — strong enough to *potentially* reach consciousness, strongly activated, but
  **lacks top-down attention** now → not currently conscious (e.g., an unattended supraliminal item
  in a buffer).
- **Conscious** — strong bottom-up **plus** top-down attention → ignition, broadcast, reportability.

> This is precisely a **two-stage gate**: (1) a bottom-up strength/salience filter (preattentive),
> then (2) an attentional selection deciding which eligible (preconscious) representation gets
> amplified into the workspace. **Strongest scientific warrant for a "subconscious gate selects what
> the conscious workspace processes" framing.**

### 4.4 Default Mode Network (DMN) — Raichle (2001)
*PNAS* 98(2):676–682. Midline + lateral regions (medial PFC, posterior cingulate/precuneus, angular
gyrus) with high resting metabolism that *decreases* during externally-directed tasks
("task-negative"). Associated with internally-directed cognition: mind-wandering, autobiographical
memory, future simulation, theory-of-mind. **Anticorrelated** with the dorsal-attention/frontoparietal
"task-positive" network (Fox et al. 2005) → a real **internal-vs-external mode switch**.

**Caution:** Mapping DMN = "subconscious" / task-positive = "conscious reasoning" is a **loose
metaphor, not established fact.** Much DMN-linked activity (mind-wandering, inner narrative) is
*consciously experienced*; the DMN contributes to conscious task content (Sormaz et al. 2018) and
overlaps GNW hubs anatomically. Best-supported reading: a genuine internal/external attentional mode
switch — useful architectural inspiration (toggling internally-generated candidate content vs
externally-driven processing), overreaching if called "the subconscious network."

### 4.5 Competing theories & the 2020s state of the debate
- **Integrated Information Theory (IIT)** — Tononi, Koch. Consciousness = maximally integrated
  information (Φ) in a system's intrinsic cause-effect structure; locates substrate in a **posterior
  "hot zone"**, needs *no* broadcast or PFC. Directly opposed to GNW.
- **Higher-Order Theories (HOT)** — Rosenthal, Lau, Brown. A state is conscious by being the object
  of a **higher-order (metacognitive) representation**; emphasizes prefrontal monitoring.
- **Cogitate Consortium (2025), *Nature*** — preregistered adversarial GNW-vs-IIT test (256 subjects;
  fMRI/MEG/iEEG). Conscious content decodable across visual, ventrotemporal, **and** inferior frontal
  cortex (challenging IIT's posterior-only claim); but **predicted GNW ignition at stimulus offset
  largely failed to appear**, and posterior sustained synchrony was lacking (challenging IIT).
  **"Substantially challenges key tenets of both theories."** No winner. (Cf. the 2023 "IIT is
  pseudoscience" open letter — the field is heated and unsettled.)

### 4.6 Bottom line for the framing
**Solid science (safe to build on):** a real distinction between parallel unconscious processing and
a capacity-limited conscious state; conscious access gated by **bottom-up strength × top-down
attention** (a genuine selection stage); nonlinear all-or-none **ignition/threshold** with a
~270–300 ms/P3b signature; winning content **broadcast globally**. The blackboard/broadcast
architecture is a legitimate, well-precedented template for a context-composition/workspace module.

**Metaphor/speculation (flag it):** no unified anatomical "subconscious agent"; DMN ≠ subconscious;
equating a functional workspace broadcast with *phenomenal* consciousness is a category leap the
science doesn't license (GWT/GNW are theories of *access*, and even those are contested — Cogitate
2025).

**Defensible synthesis to use:** "A preattentive salience/strength filter plus an attentional
selection policy gates which representations get amplified into a shared, limited-capacity broadcast
buffer that makes selected content globally available to all downstream modules" — a faithful
functional restatement of the Dehaene–Baars global-workspace framework, and an apt analogy for a
learned routing/gating policy feeding a context-composition module. Present as **functional
inspiration, not a claim of consciousness.**

## 5. What's missing? Brain systems for continual learning NOT in {thinking, LTM, STM, context composition, policy, reward}

The six modules cover the **representational** side (where info lives, how it's used) but omit most
of the **control machinery deciding WHEN, HOW FAST, and WHETHER to write to memory** — which is
exactly where the biology of continual learning lives.

### Ranked verdict
| Mechanism | Relevance | Gap it fills |
|---|---|---|
| Acetylcholine encode/consolidate switch | **HIGH** | Explicit mode-switch: read-new vs replay-consolidate |
| Norepinephrine / LC adaptive gain | **HIGH** | Uncertainty-driven learning-rate + network reset |
| Metaplasticity | **HIGH** | Per-weight write-protection = anti-forgetting |
| Catastrophic forgetting / stability-plasticity | **HIGH** (the *problem*) | The dilemma the whole design must solve |
| Homeostatic plasticity / sleep downscaling | **HIGH** | Prevent saturation; renormalize during sleep |
| Prioritized generative replay | **HIGH** | The offline write engine beyond scheduled rehearsal |
| Schema-accelerated consolidation | **HIGH** | Consolidate fast when new info fits prior structure |
| Emotional/salience tagging (amygdala) | **MED-HIGH** | Which memories deserve strong writes |
| Adult neurogenesis / pattern separation | **MEDIUM** | Interference reduction via capacity expansion |
| Serotonin | **MEDIUM** | Temporal discounting / patience / mood baseline |
| Basal-ganglia habits (beyond policy) | **MEDIUM** | Goal-directed→habitual transfer; compute amortization |
| Predictive coding / free-energy | **MEDIUM** (framing) | Surprise as universal learning driver |
| Cerebellum / forward models | **LOW** | Supervised motor calibration — not this domain |

### 5.1 Neuromodulators beyond dopamine (biggest genuine gap)
The `reward` module captures only dopamine/TD-error. The brain uses ≥3 other diffuse neuromodulators
to control the *learning process itself* — the meta-controllers a continual agent needs.

- **Acetylcholine — encode/consolidate mode switch. HIGH.** Hasselmo (2006, *Curr. Opin. Neurobiol.*
  16:710–715): **high ACh (waking/attention) → encoding** (boosts feedforward input, suppresses
  recurrent feedback, so the net responds to new input not its own priors); **low ACh (SWS) →
  consolidation** (releases recurrent feedback, enabling SWR replay). A literal dial between
  "read/write-new" and "replay/consolidate." *AI:* an explicit plasticity-vs-consolidation gain — and
  a reason to **suppress retrieval during encoding** to avoid prior-contamination (interference
  control), which "context composition" implicitly assumes always-on.
- **Norepinephrine / locus coeruleus — adaptive gain. HIGH.** Aston-Jones & Cohen (2005),
  *Annu. Rev. Neurosci.* 28:403–450. **Phasic** LC → exploitation/focus; **tonic** LC →
  exploration/disengagement. NE also does **learning-rate control** (learn fast when surprising) and
  **network reset** (large transient interrupts and reconfigures for a changed environment). Yu &
  Dayan (2005): ACh = *expected* uncertainty, NE = *unexpected* uncertainty. *AI:* dynamic
  learning-rate scheduling, exploration temperature, context-drift detection.
- **Serotonin. MEDIUM.** Leading accounts: **temporal discounting/patience**, behavioral inhibition,
  mood baseline. Least settled monoamine.
- **Doya (2002), *Neural Networks* 15:495–506** — cleanest summary of what's missing: dopamine = TD
  error, **serotonin = discount factor γ, noradrenaline = inverse temperature (exploration),
  acetylcholine = learning rate α**. The `reward` module is only the TD-error corner.

### 5.2 Metaplasticity — "plasticity of plasticity." HIGH.
The rate/sign/threshold of *future* plasticity depends on a synapse's own recent history — a hidden
per-synapse state gating learning. **BCM** (Bienenstock, Cooper & Munro 1982): the LTP/LTD threshold
**slides** with average postsynaptic activity → automatic stabilization. Abraham & Bear (1996),
*Trends Neurosci.* 19:126–130; Abraham (2008), *Nat. Rev. Neurosci.* 9:387.
- *AI:* the direct biological analog of anti-forgetting. **Elastic Weight Consolidation** (Kirkpatrick
  et al. 2017, *PNAS* 114:3521–3526) makes important weights stiffer via a Fisher-information penalty
  = a learned per-weight plasticity rate = metaplasticity. Cascade models (Fusi et al. 2005). **The
  mechanism most directly protecting LTM from catastrophic overwrite — and it is absent from the
  module list.**

### 5.3 Catastrophic forgetting / stability-plasticity dilemma. HIGH — but the *problem*, not a module.
Too plastic → new learning erases old (McCloskey & Cohen 1989); too stable → can't learn new
(Grossberg's dilemma). The brain's answer is the *portfolio* of the other mechanisms here (CLS,
metaplasticity, replay, homeostasis, neuromodulatory gating). The {STM, LTM} split ≈ CLS captures
part of it — but CLS's **interleaved replay** requirement is the usually-omitted part.

### 5.4 Homeostatic plasticity / synaptic scaling & the Synaptic Homeostasis Hypothesis. HIGH.
Hebbian learning is unstable (runaway potentiation). **Synaptic scaling** multiplicatively
renormalizes a neuron's synapses to hold firing in range while preserving relative weights
(Turrigiano 2008, *Cell* 135:422). **SHY** (Tononi & Cirelli 2014, *Neuron* 81:12–34, "Sleep and the
price of plasticity"): waking net-potentiates; **SWS downscales** to a sustainable baseline,
restoring signal-to-noise and learning capacity. *AI:* the sleep phase shouldn't *only* strengthen
(replay) — it should **also renormalize/prune** to prevent saturation. Under-appreciated,
concrete design lever. (SHY's "sleep's *primary* function is downscaling" is contested vs
active-systems-consolidation — likely both occur.)

### 5.5 Prioritized generative replay. HIGH — the biggest addition to the sleep design.
Beyond §1/§3: framed computationally, hippocampal replay is **generative replay** from a hierarchical
generative model — a leading mechanism for consolidation *and* continual learning **without storing
raw data**. Mattar & Daw (2018, *Nat. Neurosci.* 21:1609) — replay as **utility-ranked prioritized
planning** (connects to `reward`). Stoianov, Maisto & Pezzulo (2022). *AI:* "generative replay"
(Shin et al. 2017, NeurIPS; van de Ven, Siegelmann & Tolias 2020, *Nat. Commun.* 11:4069) trains a
generator to **dream** old data and interleave it with new — a well-supported cure for catastrophic
forgetting. The sleep phase should do **prioritized (Mattar-Daw-ranked) generative replay**, not just
scheduled rehearsal.

### 5.6 Schema-accelerated consolidation. HIGH — a missing consolidation-timing policy.
Normally consolidation is slow, but when new info **fits an existing schema** it is dramatically
**accelerated**: Tse et al. (2007), *Science* 316:76–82 — rats made one-trial flavor-place
associations hippocampus-independent within ~48h once a schema existed; mPFC gates schema-congruent
encoding. van Kesteren et al. (2012), *Trends Neurosci.* 35:211–219 (SLIMM model). *AI:* the
**consolidation schedule should be content-dependent** (a schema-match score) — congruent → consolidate
fast, directly into the slow store; novel/conflicting → keep in the fast/episodic store longer.

### 5.7 Emotional/salience tagging (amygdala). MEDIUM-HIGH.
McGaugh (2004), *Annu. Rev. Neurosci.* 27:1–28. BLA + stress hormones/NE gate how strongly
hippocampal/cortical traces consolidate — a **salience-weighted write-priority** *decoupled from task
reward*. *AI:* the `reward` module conflates *value-for-action* with *importance-for-storage*; biology
separates them. Add an explicit **consolidation-priority signal** (novelty + surprise + goal-relevance).

### 5.8 Adult neurogenesis / dentate-gyrus pattern separation. MEDIUM (role debated).
New granule cells continuously added to the DG; proposed to aid **pattern separation** and temporal
memory tagging/interference reduction. Aimone, Deng & Gage (2011), *Neuron* 70:589–596 (itself a
critique of loose "pattern separation" usage); Sahay et al. (2011, *Nature*). *AI:* analogous to
**dynamically expanding networks** (progressive nets, Rusu et al. 2016) / sparse-coding for
interference reduction — useful inspiration, loose mapping.

### 5.9 Basal-ganglia beyond WM gating. MEDIUM (partly in `policy`).
Two additions: **dual controllers** (dorsomedial = goal-directed/model-based vs dorsolateral =
habitual/model-free, with goal→habit transfer on overtraining; Yin & Knowlton 2006, *Nat. Rev.
Neurosci.* 7:464–476; Daw, Niv & Dayan 2005 arbitration); and the **actor-critic RL** substrate
(anatomically distinct actor/critic). *AI:* model-based/model-free arbitration + **caching expensive
deliberation into cheap habits** (compute amortization for repeated tasks).

### 5.10 Predictive coding / free-energy. MEDIUM (framing, overlaps `thinking`).
Friston (2010), *Nat. Rev. Neurosci.* 11:127 — perception/action/learning as minimizing prediction
error (surprise) under a hierarchical generative model. A unifying *framing*, largely overlapping the
world-model in `thinking`; criticized as over-general/unfalsifiable. *AI:* adopt the **principle**
(surprise-gated writes) — connects to §5.7 and NE = unexpected uncertainty.

### 5.11 Cerebellum / forward internal models. LOW for a text/decision agent.
Wolpert, Miall & Kawato (1998); Ito (2008), *Nat. Rev. Neurosci.* 9:304 — supervised forward/inverse
models for error-based motor calibration. For an ALFWorld/text-decision agent there is no continuous
motor plant to calibrate; the forward-model *idea* is already the `thinking`/world-model module.
Supervised motor adaptation specifically is **not design-relevant here.**

### 5.12 The through-line
The six modules describe **where information lives and how it's used**. They systematically omit the
**neuromodulatory/homeostatic control layer governing the learning process**:
1. **When to encode vs consolidate** → acetylcholine.
2. **How fast to learn / when to reset** → norepinephrine/LC; serotonin for discounting.
3. **Which weights to protect** → metaplasticity / EWC.
4. **Which memories deserve strong writes** → amygdala salience tagging; surprise/free-energy.
5. **What to replay offline and in what order** → prioritized generative replay.
6. **How to prevent saturation** → homeostatic scaling / sleep downscaling.
7. **How fast to consolidate given prior knowledge** → schemas.

Highest-value additions for *this* project: **metaplasticity (weight-level write protection),
prioritized generative replay (offline write engine), homeostatic renormalization/pruning in the
sleep phase, neuromodulatory learning-rate/mode control (ACh + NE), and schema-gated consolidation
timing.** Cerebellar forward models are LOW; neurogenesis and free-energy are MEDIUM and partly
redundant.
